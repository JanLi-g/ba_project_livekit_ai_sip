Summary: This research examines the Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) as an enabling tool for Small and 
Medium-sized Enterprises (SMEs) to develop and implement Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems. The 
study is justified by the need to support these companies in adopting AI-based technologies while maintaining com-
pliance with regulatory and ethical requirements, contributing academically to the integration between the General 
Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the ethical principles of respect for human autonomy, prevention of harm, 
fairness, and explainability, all established by the European Commission. This article is based on a systematic doc-
umentary analysis, examining scientific articles, normative instruments, and guidelines from Data Protection Au-
thorities within the European scenario. The results demonstrate the viability of DPIA as a precursor instrument for 
the development and implementation of Trustworthy AI systems, evidenced by the convergence between the GDPR 
and European ethics guidelines, identifying significant operational synergies. The DPIA methodology proposed in 
this article offers a framework for implementing technical and legal safeguards without compromising innovation, 
although there are structural limitations in the regulatory framework. The limitations identified indicate the need 
for future studies on the possibility of integrating DPIA with the requirements of the AI Act, in order to continually 
improve the proposed framework.

Keywords: Data Protection; Artificial Intelligence; Impact Assessment; Small and Medium-sized Enterprises; Ethical 
principles.

Introduction

The advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) has driven a significant transformation in the business land-
scape,  and  small  and  medium-sized  enterprises  (SMEs)  are  progressively  adopting  these  technologies  to  remain 
competitive.  However, this digitalization process presents  substantial challenges related to regulatory compliance 
and the implementation of ethical practices in the development and implementation of algorithmic systems. In this 
context, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) has emerged as an important regulatory framework, estab-
lishing specific requirements for the processing of personal data, including those processed by AI systems.

The complexity of this scenario intensifies when we consider the ethics guidelines established by the AI High 
Level Expert Group (AI HLEG, 2019), which defines specific criteria for the development of Trustworthy AI. These 
guidelines state that algorithmic systems must be both legal, ethical, and technically robust, presenting compliance 
streams that often challenge the operational and financial capacity of SMEs. Therefore, the intersection between data 
protection and ethical AI principles requires methodological tools to enable their implementation in the specific 
context of these organisations.

From  this  perspective,  the  Data  Protection  Impact  Assessment  (DPIA),  provided  for  in  Article  35  of  the 
GDPR, is a potential facilitating tool for SMEs to develop and implement Trustworthy AI systems. As Hijmans and 
Raab (2018) point out, there is an intrinsic relationship between the GDPR and fundamental ethical dimensions, 
manifested  through  values  such  as  human  dignity  and  personal  autonomy.  This  feature  suggests  that  the  DPIA, 
suitably adapted, can serve as an initial methodological framework for implementing Trustworthy AI requirements.
Given this, this paper investigates the extent to which DPIA can act as a facilitating instrument for SMEs to 
develop and implement Trustworthy AI systems. This question has significant social relevance considering the fun-
damental role of SMEs in the European economy and the need to support them in the regulatory context related to 
AI. From an academic point of view, the research contributes to filling a gap in the legal literature by proposing the 
integration of data protection regulatory requirements and ethical principles of Trustworthy AI.

In the end, a methodology will be proposed for SMEs to carry out DPIA in the context of AI systems, inte-
grating data protection requirements and ethical principles established by the European Commission. To achieve 
this result, three stages will be developed: (i) analysis of the ethical principles and requirements of Trustworthy AI 
established by the European Commission; (ii) identification of the fundamental elements of DPIA established by 
Article 35 of the GDPR and by applicable guidelines; and (iii) structure of specific modules for conducting the pro-
posed DPIA methodology, which are inspired by the guidelines of the Article 29 Working Party (WP29, 2017) and 
the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO, 2018, 2020), adapted to the particularities of SMEs and the specific 
requirements of AI systems.

Finally, it is worth noting that, despite the recent approval of the AI Act by the European Union, which estab-
lishes a specific regulatory framework that includes impact assessment requirements for AI systems classified as high 
risk, this work focusses on instrumentalising the DPIA as an initial and methodologically accessible mechanism for 
SMEs to develop and implement Trustworthy AI systems. This methodological option is based on three predominant 
aspects: (i) the establishment of the GDPR in the European legal system and the consequent familiarity of organisa-
tions with its instruments and basic principles; (ii) the existence of a robust body of interpretative guidelines issued 
by the Data Protection Authorities on the operationalisation of DPIA; and (iii) the possibility of taking advantage of 
organisational structures and processes already implemented in compliance with the GDPR, optimising resources 
and promoting operational efficiency in the specific context of SMEs.

1. Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence, in line with the European Commission’s Guide-
lines

AI-based systems and applications are significantly transforming contemporary legal, social, and economic 
relations, which calls for a regulatory framework that harmonises technological development with the protection of 
fundamental rights. 

In this context, the European Commission, recognising the need to establish clear and ethically orientated 
normative parameters, has presented specific guidelines for the development of Trustworthy AI systems, in which

 1.1 The Concept of a Trustworthy AI: Legal, Ethical, and Technical Components 

The publication of the Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI (Ethics Guidelines) by AI HLEG 8 April 2019 was 
a significant milestone for the development and implementation of algorithmic systems within the European Union 
(AI  HLEG,  2019). This  document  inaugurated  a  regulatory  perspective  based  on  the  integration  of  fundamental 
rights and ethical principles that should guide technological innovations.

The Ethics Guidelines consolidated a breakthrough in the systematisation of the normative framework for AI, 
based on the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union and universal human rights (AI HLEG, 2019). 
This  structure  demonstrated  the  need  to  harmonise  technological  progress  with  the  values  of  European  society, 
establishing objective criteria to safeguard individual and collective rights in the context of digital transformation.

The central purpose of the guidelines is to provide guidance for the development of AI-based systems that 
can be considered trustworthy (AI HLEG, 2019). This approach recognises that trustworthiness transcends technical 
efficiency, incorporating ethical, legal, and social aspects that must be observed throughout the system’s lifecycle. 
Consequently, the guidelines establish a structured path for developers and organisations to implement AI-based 
technologies in accordance with ethical principles and fundamental rights, promoting a human-centred and socially 
responsible technological evolution (AI HLEG, 2019).

In  this  context,  the  Ethics  Guidelines  present  a  systematic  structure  that  enables  a  gradual  understanding 
of the elements necessary for the responsible development of algorithmic systems. Chapter I establishes the funda-
mental rights and ethical principles that must be observed when developing trustworthy AI applications (AI HLEG, 
2019). This initial structure presents the premises that guide all subsequent development of the document.

Chapter II materialises the application of these fundamentals by establishing seven essential requirements for 
the development of trustworthy AI-based systems (AI HLEG, 2019). This section transposes general principles into 
specific guidelines, providing objective parameters for the implementation of ethical precepts in the development 
process. Furthermore, Chapter III presents the mechanisms for operationalising these requirements, providing prac-
tical tools for their effective implementation (AI HLEG, 2019).

As you can see, the document goes beyond a simple list of ethical principles and is an instrument that inte-
grates theory and practice in the development of AI-based technologies (AI HLEG, 2019). This approach recognises 
the complexity inherent in the process and offers comprehensive guidelines ranging from conceptual foundations 
to their respective application. The progressive framework allows organisations and developers to understand the 
building blocks of trustworthy AI and the mechanisms for its effective implementation.

The construction of a trustworthy AI is based on three components that must be developed in an integrated 
and harmonious manner. As established by AI HLEG (2019), the algorithmic system must meet three fundamental 
dimensions: be “Legal”, observing the applicable set of regulations; “Ethical”, ensuring compliance with fundamental 
values and principles; and “Solid”, ensuring technical and social consistency. These three pillars form the essential 
core for the development of AI applications that meet contemporary legal and social requirements.

It is important to note that the implementation of AI-based systems requires a comprehensive approach that 
covers all phases of the system’s life cycle (AI HLEG, 2019). Developers and organisations must constantly evaluate 
and monitor the established criteria, from initial conception to final implementation. This perspective shows that 
compliance is not restricted to a specific moment but is a continuous process of verification and adaptation to the 
guidelines. The agents responsible for development must incorporate evaluation mechanisms to ensure that stan-
dards are maintained at each stage of the process.

However,  mere  legal  compliance  is  not  enough  to  characterise  an  algorithmic  system  as  trustworthy  (AI 
HLEG, 2019). This finding highlights the multifaceted nature of reliability in AI applications, where legal, ethical and 
technical aspects are inextricably linked. The interaction between these components structures a framework that 
requires integrated analysis, recognising that each element, although necessary, is not sufficient in isolation to ensure 
effective system compliance.

Given the complex scenario surrounding algorithmic systems and the pressing need to safeguard fundamen-
tal rights in the context of digital transformation, the ethical dimension emerges as a core element in building trust-
worthy AI. Therefore, it is imperative to deepen the analysis of the ethical principles established by AI HLEG (2019), 
considering that they constitute the foundation for the development of AI systems that promote a balance between 
innovation and protection of rights. A systematic understanding of these principles helps organisations, especially 
SMEs, to implement algorithmic systems responsibly and in line with society’s fundamental values.

 1.2 The ethical principles of a trustworthy AI 

The Ethics Guidelines present four essential principles for the development of a Trustworthy AI: respect for 
human autonomy, prevention of harm, fairness, and explainability (AI HLEG, 2019). These ethical imperatives are 
based on Article 2 of the Treaty on the European Union.

These principles, even without binding force, are important in interpreting how fundamental rights should be 
protected in the face of new technologies. It is important to note that no principle is more important than another, 
and they may even conflict depending on the situation. In such cases, each case needs to be analysed individually to 
find the best solution (AI HLEG, 2019).

1.2.1 Principle of respect for human autonomy

The principle of respect for human autonomy is a core element in the architecture of technologies based on 
trustworthy AI, founded on the essential rights to individual dignity and freedom. This concept is based both on 
the constitutional protection of fundamental rights and on the very structure of the Democratic Rule of Law, which 
presupposes individual autonomy as a legitimising element of social or technological legal relations. From this per-
spective, Floridi et al. (2019) establish as a central premise the right to self-determination of individuals over the 
algorithmic treatments to which they are subject.

Realising this principle in AI applications requires a specific methodology, as established by AI HLEG (2019), 
prioritising the development of systems that leverage human competencies. This approach emphasises strengthening 
users’ cognitive and sociocultural capabilities. Floridi et al. (2019) introduce a complementary dimension by pro-
posing that, in addition to promoting human autonomy, limitations be placed on machine autonomy, guaranteeing 
reversibility mechanisms when necessary.

The materialisation of the principle of human autonomy in AI technologies requires integrated legal and tech-
nical mechanisms. From this perspective, they become instruments that ensure access to information and the exer-
cise of decision-making power over algorithmic delegations. The concept of meta-autonomy, according to Floridi et 
al. (2019), underpins this construction by determining that the preservation of human decision-making power is an 
essential element, guaranteeing both the exercise of freedom of choice and the prerogative of revocation.

1.2.2 The principle of damage prevention

The principle of preventing harm in AI technologies is based on a double normative matrix: respect for hu-
man  dignity,  as  established  in  Article  2  of  the  Treaty  on  the  European  Union  (TEU),  and  the  protection  of  the 
physical and mental integrity of individuals, provided for in Article 3 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the 
European Union (Charter). This legal framework includes a comprehensive conceptual dimension which, according 
to AI HLEG (2019), incorporates both individual and collective aspects of potential harm, including intangible im-
pacts on social, cultural, and political environments. The systematic classification proposed by Floridi et al. (2019) 
establishes a fundamental distinction between accidental harm, resulting from the excessive use of AI applications, 
and deliberate harm, resulting from the misuse of these systems.

Operationalisation of the damage prevention principle requires the implementation of specific technical and 
legal safeguards in algorithmic solutions. This preventive perspective, as established by Floridi et al. (2019), priori-
tises the mitigation of damage regardless of its origin, whether derived from human intentionality or the unforeseen 
behaviour of systems. The AI HLEG (2019) complements this approach by establishing objective technical require-
ments that determine the need to incorporate security mechanisms against malicious use, consolidating a compre-
hensive protective framework in the development and implementation of AI-based technologies.

1.2.3 The Principle of Fairness

The  principle  of  fairness  in  AI  systems  is  based  on  respect  for  human  dignity  and  the  fundamental  right 
to equality, as set out in Article 2 of the TEU. This normative basis is consolidated through a double dimension: 
substantive and procedural. From the substantive perspective, as AI HLEG (2019) points out, AI applications must 
ensure fair and equitable services, establishing safeguards against unfair bias and discriminatory practices that can 
stigmatise specific individuals or groups.

The procedural dimension of the principle of fairness finds significant support in the GDPR, especially in 
Article  22(3),  which  establishes  specific  guarantees  for  automated  individual  decisions. This  procedural  aspect  is 
intrinsically related to the principle of proportionality, according to which, as established by the AI HLEG (2019), 
when faced with multiple alternatives to achieve a given objective, priority must be given to the one that least inter-
feres with fundamental rights and established ethical standards.

AI-based technologies, by implementing the principle of fairness, indirectly promote users’ individual free-
dom. This correlation manifests itself to the extent that discriminatory or inequitable treatments directly impact the 
autonomy  of  individuals.  Thus,  algorithmic  solutions  must  incorporate  technical  and  procedural  safeguards  that 
ensure not only formal compliance with the precepts of fairness but also their effective materialisation in the devel-
opment and implementation of these technologies.

1.2.4 The principle of explainability

The principle of explainability establishes direct links with fundamental principles of the legal system, such 
as human dignity, individual freedom, and justice (AI HLEG, 2019), operating as an instrument to verify fairness in 
algorithmic systems. This interconnection of principles strengthens the right to information as an essential compo-
nent of individual autonomy, allowing subjects to understand and question the decisions that affect them. Thus, the 
transparency provided by explainability transcends mere technical compliance, becoming a fundamental guarantee 
in legal and social relations mediated by AI-based technologies.

This principle is a core element for building and maintaining user trust in AI systems, as established by AI 
HLEG (2019). This systemic dimension is consolidated as a transversal principle which, according to Floridi et al. 
(2019), complements and strengthens all the other ethical principles applicable to AI-based technologies. Explain-
ability manifests itself through a comprehensive informational dimension, which includes not only the transparency 
of algorithmic processes, but also clarity about the purposes and components of the system, especially for individuals 
directly or indirectly affected by its decisions.

The application of the explainability principle has gradations proportional to the potential impact of automat-
ed decisions. While music content recommendation systems require a lower degree of transparency, AI applications 
in the medical field, which influence diagnoses and treatments, require higher levels of explainability. This gradation 
highlights the contextual nature of the principle, which adapts to the criticality and sensitivity of different technolog-
ical applications, safeguarding fundamental rights in proportion to the risks involved.

 1.3 The Seven Essential Requirements for Implementing a Trustworthy AI 

In synergy with the ethical principles analysed in the previous topic, and in order to make them more op-
erational and concrete in the implementation of AI systems, it is necessary to examine the seven requirements that 
instrumentalise these precepts. These requirements, presented in a non-hierarchical structure, comprise: (i) human 
action and supervision; (ii) technical soundness and safety; (iii) data privacy and governance; (iv) transparency; (v) 
diversity, non-discrimination, and equity; (vi) societal and environmental well-being; and (vii) accountability. 

1.3.1 Human action and supervision 

The implementation of human action and supervision mechanisms in AI systems is supported by essential 
legal principles, including respect for human dignity, individual freedom, and autonomy. As AI HLEG (2019) points 
out, this requirement emerges from the need to safeguard individual self-determination as a structuring element of 
the normative architecture of algorithmic systems. This perspective is in line with the paradigm of human centrality,

which is a fundamental principle for the development of a technologically and ethically advanced society (European 
Commission, 2019).

The  operationalisation  of  these  principles  is  materialised  through  specific  protection  and  risk  assessment 
mechanisms. In this sense, AI HLEG (2019) establishes the imperative need to carry out impact assessments on fun-
damental rights when AI applications have the potential to negatively affect individuals or society. This preventive 
approach is complemented by a multilevel control structure that must permeate all stages of algorithmic processing, 
guaranteeing adequate degrees of supervision, adaptability, and explainability of the systems.

The implementation of human supervision mechanisms in AI systems must meet concrete and measurable 
objectives. As established by AI HLEG (2019), these technologies need to function as instruments for democratic 
empowerment and equitable social development, always preserving and promoting fundamental rights of individu-
als. This perspective emphasises the need for algorithmic systems to act as facilitators of the human decision-making 
process, allowing for more informed choices aligned with individual interests, without compromising users’ auton-
omy (European Commission, 2019).

To achieve these goals, it is essential to develop a governance model that harmonises technical aspects and 
ethical legal considerations in the supervision of AI systems (AI HLEG, 2019). This approach proves particularly im-
portant in the context of SMEs, which need clear guidelines to implement human supervision mechanisms without 
hindering their ability to innovate. The balance between control and technological development allows companies 
to adopt AI-based solutions that simultaneously respect fundamental values and promote responsible technological 
advances, thus ensuring the reliability and effectiveness of these systems in the business environment.

1.3.2 Technical soundness and safety

Technical soundness and safety in AI applications is based on the right to respect physical and mental in-
tegrity, as set out in Article 3 of the Charter, and on the ethical principle of preventing harm. The AI HLEG (2019) 
establishes four essential characteristics to meet this requirement: resilience against attacks, contingency planning, 
accuracy, and reliability with reproducibility. This preventive approach aims to ensure that AI systems work as in-
tended, minimising risks, and avoiding unacceptable harm in their operation.

Protection against vulnerabilities is a central element in the architecture of AI systems. Attacks targeting the 
structure, data, or models can compromise their functioning, generating instability, and inaccurate decisions. For 
this reason, algorithmic solutions must incorporate security and protection principles by design, implementing pro-
cesses to minimise or reverse unintended consequences (European Commission 2019).

Accuracy and reliability represent fundamental pillars for algorithmic systems, especially when they directly 
impact individuals. The AI HLEG (2019) states that reliability manifests itself when the system operates properly 
with different input data, while reproducibility ensures consistent behaviour under identical conditions. The level of 
accuracy must be proportional to the potential impact on human lives, since incorrect interpretations can result in 
unfair decisions, affecting individuals’ fundamental rights.

1.3.3 Privacy and data governance 

Privacy and data governance are emerging as fundamental elements in the development of AI systems, based 
on the right to data protection and the ethical imperative to prevent harm. This aspect gains even more relevance 
considering that the damage caused can be immaterial in nature, as threats to privacy increase when the individual is 
inserted in the context of a surveillance-based economy. As highlighted by AI HLEG (2019), modern AI applications 
are capable of inferring a wide range of sensitive information about individuals, including personal preferences, de-
mographic characteristics, and even political and religious beliefs.

To establish a solid foundation of protection, algorithmic solutions must incorporate fundamental principles 
established by the GDPR in Article 5(2). This implies ensuring data processing that is lawful, fair, and transparent, 
for a specified purpose and limited to what is necessary. According to the AI HLEG guidance (2019), this adequacy 
must be demonstrated by implementing the concepts of data protection by design and by default throughout the 
system’s life cycle.

Data quality and integrity are also crucial aspects in the governance of AI systems. The use of inaccurate, 
incorrect, or biased data can result in discriminatory decisions, compromising the fairness and equity of the system. 
In this context, organisations that process personal data need to implement robust protection measures, preventing 
unauthorised access and potential leaks that could compromise individuals’ privacy.

1.3.4 Transparency

Transparency, as a fundamental requirement for trustworthy AI applications, establishes a direct relationship 
with the ethical principle of explainability and is structured around three essential characteristics: traceability, com-
munication, and explainability itself (AI HLEG, 2019). This tripartite structure underpins not only the mechanisms 
needed to ensure that AI-based technologies operate within verifiable parameters but also establishes guidelines for 
transparent interaction with their users, expressly prohibiting these systems from presenting themselves as human 
beings, a practice that would constitute attempted confusion or deception (AI HLEG, 2019).

At the operational level, traceability emerges as a crucial element, as it makes it possible to systematically 
record and document both the decision-making process and the decisions taken by the algorithmic solutions them-
selves (European Commission 2019). This feature materialises as an auditability tool, making it possible to identify 
and understand any flaws in the decision-making process. Systematic documentation of processes not only enables 
the technical verification of systems, but also ensures the ability to explain the technical aspects and human decisions 
that permeate the entire process (AI HLEG, 2019), establishing concrete mechanisms to prevent algorithmic opacity.
The dimension of explainability takes on particular relevance in the organisational context, manifesting it-
self through transparency regarding the degree of influence that AI-based technologies exert on institutional deci-
sion-making processes (AI HLEG, 2019). This perspective intensifies especially when algorithmic systems generate 
significant impacts on the individual sphere, a circumstance in which it becomes imperative to be able to request 
adequate clarification on the decision-making process employed (AI HLEG, 2019). This approach aims to ensure 
both the effectiveness of corporate governance and the exercise of fundamental rights, especially access to justice, 
which could be undermined by the opacity of automated decision-making systems.

1.3.5. Diversity, Non-Discrimination and Equity 

Diversity, nondiscrimination, and fairness are fundamental requirements that flow from the principle of equi-
ty and are directly linked to human dignity. This requirement is established as an indispensable condition throughout 
the life cycle of AI-based technologies and is essential for the realisation of Trustworthy AI (AI HLEG, 2019).

In this context, understanding data as a portrait of society (Cortiz, 2019) reveals significant structural chal-
lenges. Data sets, often affected by historical deviations and structural discriminatory conduct, can result in unfairly 
discriminatory and prejudiced decisions against minorities and vulnerable groups when used in algorithmic sys-
tems. This reality requires specific attention in the context of SMEs.

To address these challenges, it is necessary to eliminate identifiable biases as early as the data collection phase 
(AI HLEG, 2019), through consistent governance and supervision processes. The formation of teams with different 
backgrounds, cultures, and disciplines strengthens this objective, enhancing the ability to ensure nondiscrimination 
and fairness in the development of AI applications (AI HLEG, 2019).

1.3.6 Societal and environmental well-being.

In line with the principles of fairness and harm prevention, societal and environmental well-being is a fun-
damental  requirement  that  represents  a  broad  concern  for  society,  sentient  beings,  and  the  environment,  where 
everyone must be considered as a stakeholder throughout the AI life cycle (AI HLEG, 2019). This perspective estab-
lishes that, for an AI-based technology to be considered trustworthy, it is necessary to consider its impact on the en-
vironment and sentient beings (European Commission 2019). This approach bases the development of algorithmic 
systems on the principles of sustainability and social and ecological responsibility.

As  part  of  the  requirement  for  societal  and  environmental  well-being,  AI-based  technologies  must  incor-
porate sustainability criteria and respect for the environment in their design. This guideline materialises through 
development, deployment, and use processes that prioritise less harmful choices in terms of energy consumption (AI 
HLEG, 2019). At the same time, AI applications must include systematic assessments of the impacts of their use on 
users’ social, physical, and mental skills, recognising their intrinsic social dimension (AI HLEG, 2019).

This requirement also establishes fundamental links with respect for democracy, justice, and the rule of law, 
demanding a comprehensive analysis of the impacts of AI-based technologies from a societal perspective (AI HLEG, 
2019). In this context, it becomes imperative to systematically assess the effects of these algorithmic solutions on 
democratic institutions and society in general, with special attention to democratic processes, ensuring that techno-
logical development strengthens the fundamental structures of the rule of law.

1.3.7 Accountability

Accountability, as one of the seven essential requirements outlined in the Ethics Guidelines, plays a key role 
in promoting ethical principles and robust governance practices. This requirement is intrinsically linked to fairness, 
requiring  the  implementation  of  mechanisms  that  ensure  both  effective  responsibility  and  accountability  for  the 
consequences of algorithmic solutions (European Commission 2019). The materialisation of accountability requires 
the incorporation of elements such as auditability, mitigation, and communication of adverse impacts, compromise 
solutions, and accessible remedies (AI HLEG, 2019). These components are fundamental to consolidating trust in AI 
applications, as well as to strengthening the relationship with users and other stakeholders.

As part of the instruments that enable accountability, auditability, and impact assessments occupy a promi-
nent position. Auditability enables the detailed analysis of algorithms, data, and decision-making processes, and the 
publication of audit reports contributes directly to increasing transparency and trust (AI HLEG, 2019). On the other 
hand, impact assessments play a strategic role in identifying and mitigating risks, especially in solutions based on 
personal data, as established by Article 35 of the GDPR. These tools not only document the practices adopted in the 
development of algorithmic systems but also support the adoption of corrective and preventive measures to ensure 
ethical and regulatory compliance.

Adopting the accountability requirement presents a relevant opportunity for SMEs by facilitating the imple-
mentation of solutions aligned with ethical and regulatory standards. Instruments such as impact assessments and 
independent audits can be tailored to the specific needs of these organisations, promoting competitive and respon-
sible development (AI HLEG, 2019). By incorporating these mechanisms, SMEs increase their credibility with the 
market and stakeholders, as well as ensuring that regulatory and social expectations are met, strengthening trust in 
their technological solutions.

 1.4 Challenges faced by SMEs to ensure the development of Trustworthy AI systems 

SMEs face a number of complex challenges when seeking to develop and implement AI systems that meet the 

trust criteria set by the European Union. 

According to Oldemeyer et al. (2024) and Yusuf et al. (2024), the lack of adequate technological infrastructure 
represents an initial barrier, since these companies rarely have the computing power needed to operate AI systems. 
The authors also point out that access to and management of high-quality data is a central obstacle, as SMEs often 
lack the resources to access or maintain robust data sets. Crockett et al. (2023) and Iyelolu et al. (2024) also point out 
that the shortage of qualified professionals in these organisations compromises both the development and proper 
management of these systems, creating a technical gap that is difficult to overcome.

The economic scenario presents equally significant challenges for SMEs in the context of trustworthy AI. Old-
emeyer et al. (2024), Yusuf et al. (2024) and Iyelolu et al. (2024) show that the high costs associated with implement-
ing AI systems, including investments in software, hardware, and specialised personnel, often exceed the budgetary 
capacities of these companies. Li et al. (2023) and Polemi et al. (2024) point out that this scenario becomes even more 
complex when considering the development of Trustworthy AI, which requires additional investments to guarantee 
aspects such as transparency, fairness, and privacy, elements that considerably increase the total cost of the project.

The  ethical  and  social  dimension  of  the  development  of  trustworthy  AI  represents  another  challenge  for 
SMEs. Crockett et al. (2023) and Polemi et al. (2024) note that these companies often have limitations in under-
standing and implementing the ethical implications of AI, especially on issues of algorithmic bias and data privacy. 
Li et al. (2023) emphasise that this scenario is compounded by the difficulty SMEs have in establishing public trust 
in their AI solutions, mainly due to their restricted ability to implement and demonstrate robust ethical practices in 
the development of their systems.

On the regulatory front, SMEs encounter additional challenges in implementing Trustworthy AI. Yusuf et al. 
(2024) and Iyelolu et al. (2024) identify that navigating the complex regulatory environment, especially regarding 
data protection laws and AI-specific regulations, presents an obstacle for companies with limited legal resources. 
Crockett et al. (2023) and Polemi et al. (2024) complement this analysis by pointing out that the lack of standardised 
guidelines hinders the consistent implementation of good practices. In this context, Carayannis et al. (2023) suggest 
that these obstacles can be overcome through strategic support and appropriate compliance management tools. In 
light of this, the Data Protection Impact Assessment emerges as a promising instrument to help SMEs seek compli-
ance with regulatory and ethical requirements in the development of Trustworthy AI systems.

2. The General Data Protection Regulation and its Impact Assessment

The GDPR serves as an important normative instrument in the regulation of AI systems, establishing specific 
parameters for the processing of personal data in the European context. Its relevance to the development of a Trust-
worthy AI is manifested through principles and obligations that dialogue directly with the requirements established 
for a Trustworthy AI. 

This chapter analyses the intersection between the GDPR and the development of AI systems, exploring how 
some of its provisions can contribute to the implementation of algorithmic technologies that respect both the privacy 
and fundamental rights of data subjects, in line with the ethical principles of Trustworthy AI.

 2.1 How the GDPR can help in the development of a Trustworthy AI 

The relationship between AI and the GDPR is established primarily through the very operational nature of 
algorithmic systems, especially those that rely on the processing of personal data, an activity regulated by Article 
4(2) of the GDPR. This connection is strengthened through the GDPR’s material scope of application (Art. 3(1)), 
which provides a clear framework for regulating the data collection, processing, and sharing practices that underpin 
algorithmic solutions. As studies in the field point out, this legal foundation provides a solid basis for the GDPR to 
effectively regulate AI systems that process personal data.

Furthermore, the GDPR goes beyond its regulatory function by incorporating a substantial ethical dimension 
into its structure, aligning directly with the objectives of a Trustworthy AI. As Hijmans and Raab (2018, 6) point out, 
there is a close relationship between the GDPR and ethics, manifested through fundamental moral values such as 
human dignity and personal autonomy. This ethical dimension is embodied in the basic principles of the regulation, 
especially in Article 5(1)(a), which establishes the values of lawfulness, fairness, and transparency as the guiding 
principles for data processing. Recital 4 of the GDPR reinforces this approach by stating that the processing of per-
sonal data should be designed to serve people, thus establishing a human-centered perspective for the development 
of AI systems.

AI systems present specific risks that the GDPR is structurally prepared to address. Among the most critical 
concerns are the unlawful intrusion of privacy, the unfair processing of personal data, and the use of biased data that 
can result in unjustified discrimination. The GDPR demonstrates awareness of these risks, as evidenced in Recital 
75, which expresses concern about the seriousness of the impacts on the rights and freedoms of natural persons. 
Furthermore, the GDPR incorporates values relating to human autonomy, individual freedom, fairness, and trans-
parency, as highlighted in Recitals (71) and (85), thus offering a robust framework for addressing ethical concerns 
relating to the development and implementation of AI systems.

The suitability of the GDPR as an instrument to promote the development of Trustworthy AI is based on 
both its legal legitimacy and its ethical scope. While some academics, such as Evas (2019, 6), advocate a strictly legal 
interpretation of the regulation, other scholars, such as Hijmans and Raab (2018, 6), propose a broader reading that

 2.2. The principle of responsibility and the need to demonstrate compliance in Data Processing: The 
foundations for the development of Trustworthy AI

In the context of the GDPR, the principle of responsibility emerges as one of the pillars for the processing of 
personal data. Based on Article 5(2), this principle presents a double obligation on the controller. On the one hand, 
strict compliance with the principles relating to the processing of personal data is required and, on the other, the duty 
to prove such compliance is imposed (Cordeiro 2020, 162; Kuner and Bygrave 2020, 319). This dual dimension is not 
just a regulatory formality, but a real mechanism for the effectiveness of the GDPR, since the duty to demonstrate 
compliance is treated as an independent obligation, the breach of which can result in specific liability. The Article 
29 Working Party emphasises that the purpose of this provision is to strengthen the responsibility of controllers in 
the processing of personal data, without prejudice to concrete accountability measures that may complement this 
principle (WP29, 2010).

The operationalisation of the principle of responsibility in the GDPR is manifested through various instru-
ments  and  specific  obligations.  Article  24(1)  of  the  GDPR  establishes,  as  one  of  these  manifestations,  the  imple-
mentation  of  technical  and  organisational  measures  that  must  take  into  account  “the  nature,  scope,  context,  and 
purposes of the data processing, as well as the risks to the rights and freedoms of natural persons”. Cordeiro (2020, 
321) emphasises that these measures must be interpreted broadly, covering all aspects that can influence compliance 
with the GDPR or affect the rights of data subjects. In addition to these measures, the GDPR provides for other im-
portant operational mechanisms, such as records of processing activities (Article 30), notification of data breaches 
(Article 33), and, notably, the DPIA (Article 35), which is one of the main instruments for enforcing the principle of 
accountability, especially in contexts of high-risk processing, such as AI-based systems.

The effective implementation of the principle of responsibility is intrinsically linked to a risk-based approach, 
which differs from the traditional rights-based approach. This preventive perspective is a requirement that stems 
from the fundamental right to the protection of personal data and has significantly influenced the development of 
the GDPR (Cordeiro 2020). In practice, this means that even before starting any project or initiative, the data con-
troller must assess the risks, impacts, and possible consequences that the processing of data could have on the data 
subjects. This preliminary analysis is not a mere formality: it is what allows the controller to be truly able to apply the 
appropriate measures in the specific case, whether they are technical, organisational, or, when necessary, carrying 
out a DPIA. The GDPR requires this preventive approach precisely because it recognises that the effective protection 
of personal data cannot depend solely on reactive measures but must be anchored in an organisational culture of 
prevention and continuous risk management.

The application of the risk-based approach takes on specific contours in the environment of AI systems, si-
multaneously laying the foundations for regulatory compliance and the development of Trustworthy AI. The GDPR 
recognises the particularities of this technological context by requiring a differentiated assessment for automated 
data processing, based on the inherent complexity of these technologies and their potential to generate significant 
impacts on the rights and freedoms of data subjects. 

The unique characteristics of AI systems - such as the ability to process large volumes of data, identify patterns, 
and make automated decisions - demand a multifaceted analysis that considers both technical and organisational 
aspects. Therefore, this analysis, when structured through the principle of responsibility, converges with the require-
ments of a Trustworthy AI, establishing a standard of governance that favours transparency and accountability. 

In this context, DPIA emerges as a central instrument, not only for the GDPR compliance, but as a tool for or-
ganisations to implement and demonstrate ethical practices in AI development, thus creating an operational bridge 
between data protection requirements and the principles of Trustworthy AI.

 2.3 The Data Protection Impact Assessment: Analysis of Article 35 of the GDPR 

The DPIA, established by Article 35 of the GDPR, is a fundamental instrument for protecting the rights and 
freedoms of personal data subjects. According to the WP29 guidelines (2017), the DPIA should be understood as 
a “living and continuous document”, which requires constant updating throughout the data processing cycle. This 
dynamic characteristic manifests itself in three distinct and complementary phases: project initiation, development, 
and execution, guaranteeing a complete assessment that is adaptable to changes in processing activities. 

Updating the DPIA becomes especially relevant in contexts where data processing is dynamic and subject to 
permanent changes, as highlighted by the WP29 (2017). This approach is reinforced by Article 35(11) of the GDPR, 
which establishes the possibility for the controller to carry out periodic checks to verify that the processing complies 
with the assessment initially carried out. As evidenced by the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO, 2018), the 
conduct of DPIA should be geared toward minimising and determining acceptable levels of risk in the specific cir-
cumstances of each processing, thus establishing a continuous process of evaluation and adequacy.

To operationalise this dynamic data protection instrument, the GDPR establishes a clear structure of respon-
sibilities and participation. According to Article 35(2) of the GDPR, the DPIA must be performed by the controller, 
who can choose to conduct the assessment internally or use experts outside the organisation. This flexibility in exe-
cution is complemented by the possibility of consulting the Data Protection Officer (DPO), when appointed, which 
results in an additional layer of expertise to the process. 

A particularly relevant aspect of this participatory structure is found in Article 35(9) of the GDPR, which 
provides for the possibility of consulting data subjects or their representatives about the planned processing. This 
provision significantly strengthens informational self-determination, allowing data subjects to express their opinion 
on the form and consequences of the processing of their own data. It is important to note that, according to the WP29 
(2017) guidelines, if the controller chooses not to consult the data subjects, they must formally justify the reasons for 
this decision, thus guaranteeing the transparency of the decision-making process.

Based  on  the  structure  of  responsibilities  established,  the  GDPR  defines  in  Article  35(7)  the  essential  re-
quirements that must be met when draughting a DPIA. This methodology is structured in four elements: first, a 
systematic description of the processing operations envisaged, including purposes and legitimate interests; second, 
an assessment of the necessity and proportionality of the operations in relation to the objectives established; third, 
an assessment of the risks to the rights and freedoms of data subjects; and fourth, the measures envisaged to address 
these risks, including guarantees and security procedures. 

As clarified by the ICO (2018), although the responsible party does not have the duty to completely eliminate 
risks, the conduct of the DPIA should seek to minimise them to acceptable levels in the specific circumstances. An 
important methodological aspect, highlighted by WP29 (2017), is that the entire assessment should be carried out 
from the perspective of the data subject, not the organisation, thus reinforcing the commitment to effective protec-
tion of fundamental rights. This methodological approach allows the controller to have the necessary flexibility to 
deepen the analysis according to the specifics of each case, always keeping the focus on protecting the rights of the 
data subjects.

Once the methodology described has been applied, if the person responsible for processing identifies that the 
measures planned to mitigate the risks are inadequate or insufficient, the situation of residual risks is configured. As 
WP29 (2017, 22) clarifies, these risks are characterised when (i) they do not exist, (ii) they are not known, or (iii) 
the appropriate measures have not been properly applied in view of the high risks identified in the DPIA. In these 
situations, Article 36(1) of the GDPR establishes the obligation to consult the supervisory authority before starting 
processing. 

The  prior  consultation  process  gives  the  supervisory  authority  significant  powers  under  Article  58  of  the 
GDPR, ranging from issuing written guidelines to imposing more severe measures. These include the possibility of 
ordering the controller to implement specific measures to comply with the provisions of the GDPR (Article 58(2)
(d)) or, in more serious cases, imposing a temporary or definitive limitation on processing, or even prohibiting it 
(Article 58(2)(f)). This administrative oversight mechanism ensures that, regardless of the solution adopted, whether 
adequacy or cessation of processing, the rights and interests of data subjects remain protected.

The effectiveness of the assessment and prior consultation process is intrinsically linked to the transparency 
aspects of the DPIA. Although WP29 (2017) encourages the publication of a summary or conclusions of the assess-

3. DPIA Methodology for Trustworthy AI Systems in SMES

The need for a methodology for carrying out a DPIA on AI systems emerges not only from the lack of con-
crete GDPR guidelines but mainly from the challenges faced by SMEs aiming to develop Trustworthy AI systems. As 
pointed out in Article 35 of the GDPR, although fundamental requirements are established, the regulatory text only 
provides general guidelines on how to carry out this assessment. This feature, coupled with the limited resources of 
SMEs and the complexity of AI systems, makes it necessary to develop a simplified methodology that can help these 
companies carry out DPIAs.

The methodology proposed here was developed from a systematic analysis of the main existing guidelines 
on the subject, notably those provided by the Article 29 Working Party and the Information Commissioner’s Office 
(WP29, 2018; ICO, 2018, 2020). A distinctive aspect of this proposal is its ability to simultaneously harmonise the 
risk-based approach and the rights-based approach, offering a way for controllers to develop their own assessments, 
considering that there is no single or mandatory methodology determined by the control authorities.

The methodological structure is organised into seven sequential and interconnected steps: (i) identification 
of the need to carry out a DPIA; (ii) systematic description of processing operations; (iii) consideration of consul-
tation with third parties; (iv) assessment of necessity and proportionality; (v) identification and assessment of risks; 
(vi) definition of mitigation measures; and (vii) documentation and assessment of the need for prior consultation. 
This framework has been developed to cover all the requirements of the GDPR, based on the ICO guidelines (2020) 
and WP29 (2018), with specific adaptations that reflect an interpretation geared towards the needs of SMEs in the 
development of Trustworthy AI systems.

The approach developed was specifically designed to help SMEs develop Trustworthy AI systems, balancing 
data protection needs with the practical limitations of these companies. The approach adopted seeks to simplify the 
impact assessment process without compromising its effectiveness, allowing SMEs to develop and implement AI 
systems that are both innovative and respectful of the fundamental rights of data subjects, thus strengthening their 
competitive position in the technology market.

 3.1 Criteria to determine the need for DPIA

Determining the need for a DPIA is particularly important in the context of AI systems, where the automated 
processing of personal data is intrinsic to their operation. According to Article 35(1) of the GDPR, a DPIA becomes 
necessary when processing is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of data subjects, a situation 
often present in AI systems due to their capacity for large-scale processing and analysis. To assess this need in the 
specific context of AI systems, two stages of analysis were established.

The first stage is based on the nine criteria developed by WP29, based on various references in the GDPR, 
including Article 35(3) itself, as well as Article 22 and Recitals (71), (75) and (91), a system that reflects the complex-
ity inherent in assessing risks in the specific context of AI systems and the automated processing of personal data. 

For  AI  systems,  a  DPIA  is  essential  when  data  processing  has  at  least  two  of  the  following  characteristics 

(WP29, 2018): 

(i) evaluation or classification operations through forecasting and profiling; 
(ii) automated decisions with significant legal or similar effects; 
(iii) observation, monitoring, or control of holders, or systematic control of areas accessible to the public; 
(iv) processing sensitive or highly personal data; 
(v) large-scale data processing; 
(vi) combining data sets; 
(vii) data from vulnerable data subjects, such as children and incapacitated persons, or from relationships 

where there is an imbalance of power, such as in the labour or consumer sphere; 

(viii) use of new technological or organisational solutions; or 
(ix) preventing holders from exercising rights or using services.

The second stage, based on Article 35(4) of the GDPR, refers to the mandatory consultation of the lists drawn 
up  by  the  supervisory  authorities  of  each  Member  State  where  the  AI  system  will  be  implemented.  This  step  is 
essential because certain types of processing, even if they do not present two or more characteristics of the WP29 
criteria, may represent inadmissible risks from the perspective of the competent supervisory authority. Therefore, if 
data processing is included in the list published by the supervisory authority, it becomes mandatory to carry out the 
DPIA, regardless of the previous analysis.

If it is concluded that a DPIA is not necessary after analysing the two steps, the controller must document 
this decision with detailed reasons (ICO, 2018). This documentation must include the opinion of the DPO, when 
appointed, as required by Article 35(2) of the GDPR. 

On the other hand, if there are still doubts about the qualification of the risk, it is recommended to carry out 
the DPIA as a preventive measure (WP29, 2018; ICO, 2018), thus adopting a cautious stance in protecting the rights 
and freedoms of data subjects processed by AI systems.

In the context of SMEs, in order to make this process more accessible and efficient, it is suggested the adoption 
of a structured approach that combines practical tools with collaborative support. Initially, SMEs can benefit from 
a simplified checklist that converts the nine criteria of WP29 into a practical checklist, complemented by concrete 
examples and use cases relevant to their sector. This tool can be integrated into a visual decision-making flow chart 
that guides company decisions through the evaluation process with “yes/no” decision points, reducing the complex-
ity of the analysis. Furthermore, to create/strengthen a support ecosystem, it is recommended to establish sectoral 
experience sharing networks where SMEs can exchange practical knowledge about the implementation of DPIAs 
and discuss common challenges. This measure simplifies the process of determining the need for DPIAs, but also 
promotes  a  culture  of  collaborative  compliance  among  SMEs,  allowing  them  to  optimise  their  limited  resources 
while maintaining adherence to regulatory requirements.

 3.2 Systematic mapping of operations 

The systematic description of processing operations represents a key element of the DPIA, as established by 
Article 35(7)(a) of the GDPR. This description must: demonstrate the breadth of what is to be analysed by the DPIA 
and enable the controller to provide useful information about the automated processing. In this context, it is essential 
that the controller makes clear “how” and “why” it will use AI systems to process the data, providing detailed infor-
mation on the nature, scope, context, and purposes of the processing.

The nature of the processing comprises the set of operations that the controller plans to carry out with the 
personal data. According to the ICO (2018), this includes specifying in detail how the data are collected, stored and 
used, who has access to these data and with which third parties it is shared (including processors), what retention 
period is established, what security measures are applied, and whether new technologies or new types of processing 
are implemented. 

As for the scope of the processing, the controller must indicate aspects such as the specific nature of the per-
sonal data, the presence of special category data, the volume and variety of the data, the extent and frequency of the 
processing, its duration, the number of data subjects involved, and the geographical area covered. This information 
is fundamental for the subsequent assessment of the necessity and proportionality of processing.

The context of processing, in turn, covers internal and external factors that may affect the expectations of data 
subjects or have a negative impact on their rights. The ICO (2018) establishes that the controller must specify essen-
tial elements such as the source of the data, the level of control that individuals have over their information and their 
expectations regarding the processing. It is also necessary to identify whether there are children or other vulnerable 
people among the data subjects, and to verify compliance with approved codes of conduct, as provided for in Article 
35(8) of the GDPR, or other certifications that the controller deems effective to ensure adequate data protection.

Finally, the mapping must address the purposes of the processing and the specificities of AI systems. Accord-
ing to the ICO (2018), the controller must clearly indicate what the intended result of the processing is, what the 
expected benefits are for both the controller and society, and give reasons for the legitimate interest in processing, 
where applicable. In the specific context of AI applications, the ICO (2020) stipulates that the phases in which auto-
mated processes may have an effect on the lives of data subjects must be recorded in the DPIA, including explana-
tions of relevant variations or margins of error in system performance that may affect the fairness of the processing. 
It is equally important to document the degree of human involvement in the decision-making process and at what 
point it occurs, thus ensuring a comprehensive understanding of the functioning of the algorithmic solutions im-
plemented.

 3.3 Stakeholder consultation process 

Article 35(9) of the GDPR states that the controller may request the opinion of data subjects or their represen-
tatives on aspects involved in the data processing. In practical terms, when this consultation takes place, the opinions 
gathered must be duly documented. However, if the controller believes that this consultation is not appropriate, they 
must give reasons for their decision and document this in the DPIA (WP29 2018, 17-18). 

Furthermore, in cases where data are shared with processors, their participation or advice becomes essential, 

ensuring that the DPIA has all the necessary viewpoints for a comprehensive assessment.

Finally, the ICO (2018) recommends that during the DPIA there is specialised legal support and advice, as 
well as the participation of independent experts in areas such as IT, sociology, or ethics. This participation is im-
portant, as these experts will point out possible risks to holders from a multidisciplinary perspective. This diversity 
of views makes it possible to identify potential impacts that could go unnoticed under a one-dimensional analysis. 

Recognising that hiring multiple experts can be financially unfeasible for SMEs, an effective alternative is to 
establish partnerships with civil society organisations working in the field of law and technology. These entities often 
have multidisciplinary researchers and experience in protecting the rights of data subjects, offering valuable perspec-
tives on the possible social, cultural, and ethical impacts of data processing. This approach allows SMEs to carry out 
a comprehensive and meaningful DPIA, even with limited resources, while ensuring adequate consideration of the 
rights and interests of data subjects.

 3.4 Evaluation of Necessity and Proportionality

Implementing  AI  systems  for  the  automated  processing  of  personal  data  requires  a  solid  justification  that 
goes beyond mere technological availability. As established by the ICO (2020), it is essential to prove the existence of 
a concrete problem to be solved and to demonstrate that AI represents a sensible solution to this issue. This careful 
approach is justified by the inherent risks of AI systems in the context of processing personal data, requiring a prior 
analysis to validate the real need for their implementation.

Once the initial need has been established, and following the guidelines of WP29 (2018), the DPIA must con-
sider two complementary perspectives: (i) the necessity and proportionality of the processing, and (ii) the protection 
of the rights of data subjects.

Regarding the first perspective, the analysis of the necessity and proportionality of the processing of personal 
data requires the controller to examine the following criteria: whether the purposes are specific, explicit, and legiti-
mate; whether the data are adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary; whether the processing respects the 
correct legal bases; whether the processing does not go beyond its purpose; and whether the accuracy and quality of 
the data are being guaranteed (WP29, 2018; ICO, 2018). 

Specifically, with regard to the use of AI systems for automated processing of personal data, the ICO (2020) 
clarifies that the DPIA must provide reasons to justify this choice over less risky and intrusive alternatives, where 
they exist. This requirement becomes even stricter in cases where the processing is based on the public interest or 
legitimate interests, requiring an exceptionally robust justification. In such situations, it is essential to demonstrate 
not only the necessity of the automated processing, but also its proportionality in relation to the objectives pursued.
As for the second perspective, related to protecting the rights of data subjects, the implementation of AI sys-
tems requires an assessment of the reasonable expectations of data subjects regarding the processing of their personal 
data. As guided by ICO (2020), it is essential to analyse the reasonable expectation of data subjects to have their data 
processed by AI systems. This assessment must be conducted in accordance with the principle of transparency set out 
in Article 5(1)(a) of the GDPR, which underpins information duties and guides data processing in the context of AI.
To operationalise this protection, the development of AI systems requires a clear definition of operational 
procedures to guarantee the rights of data subjects. According to the WP29 (2018) guidelines, data controllers must 
establish: methodologies for providing information to individuals (articles 12, 13 and 14), management of access and 
portability requests (articles 15 and 20), execution of data rectification and erasure processes (articles 16, 17 and 19), 
and operationalisation of the rights to object and limit processing (articles 18 and 21).

Last but not least, the compliance of AI systems also requires the implementation of complementary protec-
tion measures. The ICO (2018) emphasises the need for safeguards for international data transfers. Furthermore, 
Article 22(3) of the GDPR establishes the obligation of processes that guarantee human intervention in automated 
decisions, ensuring that the data subject has the right to challenge and review these decisions.

 3.5 Identification and assessment of risks

Identifying and assessing risks to the rights of data subjects (Article 35(7)(c) of the GDPR), when carried out 
in the context of AI systems, requires a comprehensive interpretation that goes beyond a singular view of the right 
to privacy and the protection of personal data. This analysis must consider all types of potential damage - physical, 
emotional, or material - resulting directly or indirectly from a personal information processing operation. This per-
spective is supported by the GDPR itself, which, according to Recital (04) and the WP29 guidelines (2017), establish-
es a direct relationship with the fundamental rights of the Charter, requiring an assessment that takes into account 
both direct and indirect impacts on these rights.

In  the  process  of  identifying  specific  risks,  ICO  (2018)  establishes  a  comprehensive  set  of  categories  that 
should be considered in the context of data processing by AI applications. These categories range from aspects such 
as the ability to exercise rights and access services to more specific issues such as discrimination, loss of control over 
personal data, behavioural conditioning or manipulation, identity theft, financial and reputational damage, physical 
damage, and the possibility of reidentification of pseudonymized data. This assessment should consider not only the 
direct impacts on data protection, but also the indirect repercussions that the processing of personal data may have 
on other rights.

 3.6 Risk mitigation measures 

The process of defining mitigating measures, as set out in Article 35(7)(d) of the GDPR, requires a structured 
approach, which begins with a detailed record of the sources of each risk identified in the previous stage of the DPIA. 
Only once the source of the risk is clearly understood, will the controller be able to seek appropriate measures to 
mitigate or eliminate it. This direct relationship between the source of the risk and the mitigating measure is funda-
mental, as the effectiveness of the actions implemented depends directly on their alignment with the specific nature 
of the cause of the risk identified.

Effective implementation of mitigation measures requires a multidisciplinary approach, considering that dif-
ferent sources of risk may require different technical or organisational measures. In this context, it is essential that 
the person responsible for managing these risks puts together a team of experts who can propose the appropriate 
measures, integrating professionals from technical and legal fields to ensure the effectiveness of the proposed solu-
tions. This integration enables a comprehensive risk assessment and the development of more robust and complete 
mitigation strategies.

However, in addition to the above-mentioned approach, the ICO (2018) presents a non-exhaustive set of measures 
that can be implemented, including reducing data collection, reducing the scope of processing, reducing retention 
periods, and implementing additional security measures. In addition, it recommends training those involved, using 
anonymisation or pseudonymization techniques, defining internal policies, and implementing systems to make it 
easier for data subjects to exercise their rights. The ICO (2020) also emphasises the importance of creating mecha-
nisms that allow holders to opt out of automated decisions, thus strengthening the protection of fundamental rights.

 3.7 Documentation and prior consultation process 

Documenting the results of the DPIA requires the person responsible for handling it to follow a series of 
systematic procedures. As established by the ICO (2018), it is necessary to record the additional measures for miti-
gating or eliminating the risks identified, classifying them as “eliminated”, “reduced”, or “accepted”. Additionally, any 
residual risks for which it has not been possible to define appropriate treatment measures must be identified.

The identified risks do not have to be completely eliminated, and some risks can be accepted after a propor-
tionality analysis. According to ICO (2018), this analysis should consider the relationship between the risks identi-
fied, the expected benefits of processing, and the practical difficulties of mitigation. Proper documentation of this 
proportionality assessment is essential to justify the acceptance of any residual risks. However, the existence of a high 
residual risk requires prior consultation with the supervisory authority, as required by Article 36(1) of the GDPR. 

The DPO plays an important role in this process and is required to issue an opinion on the DPIA when this 
professional is appointed, in accordance with Article 35(2) of the GDPR. If the opinion indicates that the assessment 
does not comply with the GDPR or is against the processing, the DPO may agree or disagree with the position, pro-
vided that he/she adequately substantiates his/her decision in the event of disagreement.

In addition to the opinion of the DPO, it is necessary to properly manage objections from data subjects con-
sulted during the process. In this regard, the controller must provide reasoned justifications whenever it decides to

4. Limitations of the Data Protection Regulatory Framework for DPIA in Trustworthy 
AI Systems

The analysis of DPIA in the specific context of this article reveals structural limitations in the current regula-
tory framework that may compromise its effectiveness as a risk governance instrument in Trustworthy AI systems.

The normative dimension of DPIA has its first structural limitation in the absence of a legal duty to publish 
the results. The WP29 (2017) expressly recommends the disclosure, although partial, of the conclusions obtained, 
but  this  measure  remains  a  mere  option  for  the  person  responsible  for  processing. This  normative  configuration 
compromises both the possibility of social control and the development of consolidated sectoral practices, especially 
given the potential impact of AI applications on fundamental rights.

The absence of specific criteria to assess bias and algorithmic discrimination is another structural limitation. 
Recital (75) of the GDPR expressly sets out the risks of discrimination, but there is a lack of objective parameters for 
assessing and measuring biases in AI-based technologies. This omission can result in methodologically inconsistent 
risk assessments.

The third limitation relates to the question of the explainability of automated decisions. Specialised doctrine 
has not yet reached a consensus on the extent of the right to explanation under the GDPR, particularly with regard 
to the possibility of requiring specific explanations for individual decisions (Wachter et al. 2016; Sartor 2020). This 
dogmatic vagueness could compromise the effectiveness of DPIA as an instrument of algorithmic transparency in 
algorithmic solutions.

Final Considerations

This paper investigated the viability of DPIA as a facilitating tool for SMEs to develop and implement Trust-
worthy AI systems. Through systematic documentary  analysis  of normative  instruments, scientific articles, insti-
tutional  guidelines  from  the  European  Union,  and  guidelines  from  data  protection  authorities,  the  convergence 
between the requirements of the GDPR and the ethical principles established for Trustworthy AI was examined. 

The  research  was  motivated  by  the  significant  challenges  faced  by  SMEs  in  developing  and  implementing 
Trustworthy AI systems, including human and financial resource constraints, difficulties in understanding ethical 
issues and the complexity of the regulatory environment. The central question sought to understand how the DPIA 
could act as a facilitating instrument in this context, considering its already consolidated structure in the European 
legal system and its potential ability to integrate data protection requirements with ethical AI principles.

 The results demonstrated the viability of the DPIA as a precursor methodological tool for implementing the 
requirements of trustworthy AI, based on the convergence of principles between the GDPR and European ethics 
guidelines. The research resulted in the development of a modular methodology that includes objective criteria for 
determining the need for an DPIA, systematised procedures for mapping processing operations, simplified mecha-
nisms for consulting stakeholders, and frameworks for identifying and mitigating risks, adapted whenever possible 
to the operational particularities of SMEs.

Despite the progress made, the research identified significant structural limitations in the regulatory frame-
work for the protection of personal data that deserve attention. The absence of a legal duty to publicize the results of 
the DPIA compromises social control and the development of consolidated sectoral practices. The lack of objective 
parameters in the GDPR for assessing bias and algorithmic discrimination can result in methodologically inconsis-
tent analyses. In addition, the dogmatic vagueness regarding the extent of the right to explanation under the GDPR 
presents a challenge to the effective achievement of the ethical principle of explainability in Trustworthy AI systems. 
However, despite its limitations, this work offers significant contributions to both the theoretical and practical fields. 
In the academic field, it presents a systematic analysis of the convergence between data protection and AI ethical 
principles, filling an important gap in the legal literature. From a practical perspective, it provides SMEs with an ac-
cessible and structured methodological framework for developing Trustworthy AI systems, taking into account their

for SMEs to comply with the AI Act and its requirements for developing and implementing AI systems is suggested

DPIA on AI systems could significantly facilitate their future compliance with the AI Act, highlighting the growing 
importance of this instrument in the European regulatory landscape for emerging technologies.
