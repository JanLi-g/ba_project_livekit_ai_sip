Wijethilake , Chandhi  
Ethical challenges in utilizing Artificial Intelligence in banking operations: A 
systematic review  
Jyväskylä: University of Jyväskylä, 2025 , 83 pp. 
Information Systems, Master’s Thesis  
Supervisor: Vuorinen , Jukka  
This research  examines the ethical challenges associated with the adoption of 
Artificial Intelligence (AI) in banking operations through a systematic literature 
review (SLR). The review synthesizes findings from 72 peer -reviewed journal 
articles and conference papers published between 2018 - 2025, following a rig-
orous selection process based on PRISMA guidelines. The analysis reveals that, 
while AI technologies have transformed banking by enhancing operational effi-
ciency, risk management, and personalized services, they also raise significant 
ethical concerns. Key ethical challenges identified  according to the problemati-
zation include data privacy and security issues , algorithmic bias and discrimi-
nation, deficiencies in transparency, and accountability issues in AI -driven de-
cision -making processes.  
To comprehensively evaluate these challenges, the study employs a multi -
theoretical framework that integrates ethical theories ( utilitarianism and virtue 
ethics) to assess the normative implications, the Unified Theory of Acceptance 
and Use of Technology (UTAUT) to explore stakeholder perceptions and tech-
nology acceptance, and Actor -Network Theory (ANT) to elucidate the dynamic 
interrelations  along with the translation  among  huma n and non -human actors 
in the banking ecosystem. Although the existing literature broadly acknowl-
edges  the existence of  these ethical issues, most studies tend to discuss them 
superficially without detailed analysis or practical mitigation strategies.  
Based on the synthesized evidence, this study  proposes a novel conceptual 
framework promoting  ethical AI adoption in banking. The framework high-
lights the interdependencies of ethical challenges among technical, ethical, and 
regulatory dimensions and provides a basis for future research aimed at devel-
oping more comprehensive ethical guidelines and governance standards. Ulti-
mately, the study contributes valuable insights for both academia and practice, 
promoting responsible and ethically sound AI implementation in the financial 
sector.  
  
Keywords: ethical challenges , artificial intelligence , banking

1 INTRODUCTION  
Humans have long been regarded as the primary embodiment of intelligence; 
however, with the advancement of technology and the evolution of artificial 
intelligence, this notion is increasingly being challenged. As AI systems contin-
ue to develop and exhibit cognitive capabilities traditionally associated with 
human intelligence, it becomes imperative to critically assess and redefine the 
concept of intelligence in the contemporary era. Artificial Intelligence (AI) can 
be named the most disruptive technolog y in recent history, fundamentally 
transforming various industries and everyday life. While the integration of AI 
in banking and finance has yielded considerable operational and strategic bene-
fits, it has simultaneously introduced a range of ethical challenges. From a hu-
manitarian standpoint, these ethical implications warrant rigorous academic 
attention. This study seeks to examine the ethical challenges related with AI 
adoption in the banking sector, as reflected in the current academic literature 
within a defined temporal scope. Through a systematic analysis and synthesis 
of secondary data sourced from reputable academic databases, the study aims 
to develop and propose a new  conceptual approach to fostering ethical AI 
adoption in banking institutions.  
Since AI’s formal introduction in 1956 by the American  computer scientist 
John McCarthy who initially coined the term, AI has evolved significantly, 
shaping modern technological advancements. McCarthy defined AI as  the sci-
ence and engineering of making intelligent machines , (McCarthy, 1959)  a concept 
that has since expanded to encompass the automation of human cognitive pro-
cesses through autonomous systems capable of problem -solving and decision  
making (Siemens et al., 2022)  AI spans  across a vast range of applications and 
disciplines. Since AI requ ires sophisticated computation,  decreasing cost in 
hardware  and dedicated AI chip design has made it more feasible to utilize. The 
expansion of  cloud -based services related to AI has also made it much more attainable 
for organisations would otherwise be hesitant  (Collins et al., 2021) .  
As a disruptive technology, AI has rapidly transformed the business and 
financial sectors. Its widespread adoption is driven by several key advantages, 
including automation of repetitive tasks, the ability to perform complex anal-

However, alongside these advancements, concerns have emerged regard-

should be embedded in AI models (Belenguer, 2022; Hale, 2025; NBER, 2019;

ly critical in the banking and financial sector, which operates in a highly com-
petitive and rapidly evolving technological landscape. The acceleration of AI -
driven financial products and services necessitates a careful examination of the 
ethical dimensions of AI implementation.  
Despite the practical necessity of extensively examining AI utilization in 
the banking sector, this research holds strong academic relevance by addressing 
critical gaps in the existing literature. While prior studies have acknowledged 
the ethical challenges associated with AI adoption across various sectors (Cath, 
2018; Milossi et al., 2021; Manoj Kumar et al., 2023; Adeyelu et al., 2024; Chopra, 
2024; Nguyen et al., 2024) , they often lack a systematic and comprehensive 
analysis of how these challenges are categorized, evaluated, and addressed. 
Many studies focus on specific ethical concerns, such as bias or privacy, but few 
offer an overarching framework that synthesizes these challenges in a struc-
tured manner. Furthermore, much of the existing literature approaches ethical 
AI either from a technical or regulatory perspective, with limited research ex-
ploring the interplay  and the intensified enga gement  between ethical consi dera-
tions, organizational decision -making, and customer trust in the financial sector. 
This gap creates uncertainty regarding how banks can implement AI in a way

2 THEORETICAL FOUNDATION  
Establishing a robust theoretical foundation is essential to systematically ana-
lyse the ethical challenges accompanied  with AI adoption in the banking sector. 
Given the interdisciplinary nature of this study, a comprehensive conceptual 
framework is necessary to integrate perspectives from  technology , ethics , and 
financial institutions. AI, as  non-human technology -based  system , interacts 
with established social norms and ethical expectations, particularly in the high-
ly regulated financial sector. This study aims to bridge the potential gap be-
tween AI technology adoption and its ethical implications by identifying the 
ethical interrelations  and tensions  among these three interconnected domains: 
technology, ethicality, and financial organizations.  
2.1 Multi-theoretical appr oach  
Due to the complex and often obscure  nature of these relationships, a theoreti-
cal perspective is required to uncover underlying structures and dynamics. This 
study employs three distinct yet interrelated theoretical approaches: ethical 
theories; utilitarianism and virtue ethics, technology acceptance theory UTAUT, 
and socio -technical network theory; ANT. These perspectives collectively facili-
tate a structured analysis of AI utilization in banking, ensuring alignment with 
ethical principles while considering technological feasibility and institutional 
governance.  
In this study, utilitarianism and virtue ethics illuminate the moral trade -
offs and character -based responsibilities inherent in AI -driven banking deci-
sions  and operations ; UTAUT highlights the organizational and social drivers 
of technology acceptance; and ANT reveals the socio -technical interactions 
among human and non -human actors.  While these frameworks could be ap-
plied in far greater depth -drawing, for example, on comprehensive philosophi-
cal treatments of virtue ethics  or advanced ANT studies of techno logy networks 
(Latour, 2005) , this thesis deliberately employs only those facets that directly

2.1.1 Ethical theories: Utilitarianism and Virtue Ethics  
Ethical theories provide a fundamental lens for evaluating the normative and 
moral implications of AI adoption in banking. The ethicality of AI -driven deci-
sions and operations depends not only on legal compliance but also on broader 
moral justifications that shape societal perceptions of fairness, accountability, 
and trust. This study employs Utilitarianism and Virtue Ethics  as discussed by 
Baumane -Vitolina , Cals & Sumilo  (2016)  as the primary ethical lenses to assess 
AI-related ethical concerns in banking . 
“Utilitarianism, a consequentialist ethical theory, asserts that the morality 
of an action is determined by its overall contribution to the greatest good for the 
greatest number ” (Bentham, 1994) . In the context of AI adoption in banking, 
utilitarianism helps evaluate whether AI -driven decisions lead to optimal socie-
tal benefits, balancing efficiency gains against potential ethical risks such as bias, 
data privacy violations, or unfair lending practices.  
AI adoption in banking is largely motivated by the pursuit of enhanced ef-
ficiency, cost reduction, and improved customer experience. According to utili-
tarian principles, the implementation of AI in areas such as credit scoring, fraud 
detection, customer service automation, and risk management should maximize 
benefits for both financial institutions and their clients. However, utilitarianism 
also raises ethical dilemmas when AI -driven decisions disproportionately dis-
advantage certain groups , such as marginalized communities  by reinforcing 
existing biases in lending, financial risk assessment, or customer profiling ac-
cording to a survey by National Bureau of Economic Research  in 2019  (NBER, 
2019) . Therefore, this study critically examines the trade -offs between AI -driven 
benefits and its unintended ethical consequences . 
Virtue Ethics, attributed to Aristotle, focuses on moral character rather 
than the consequences of actions (Baumane -Vitolina et al., 2016) . It emphasizes 
the development of virtues such as fairness, honesty, integrity, and responsibil-
ity in decision  making. Applying this theory to AI in banking highlights the 
necessity for ethical AI governance, where financial institutions and AI devel-
opers are expected to embody virtuous behaviours  in designing and imple-
menting AI systems.  Virtue ethics are particularly relevant in addressing ac-
countability and transparency in AI decision -making. Unlike utilitarian ap-
proaches that assess ethicality based on outcomes, virtue ethics emphasizes the 
moral responsibility of AI developers, financial institutions, and regulators in 
ensuring AI -driven decisions align with ethical values. For instance, if an AI -
based credit scoring system results in unfair loan denials, a virtue ethics per-
spective would demand accountability from the institutions deploying the

and application.  
By integrating utilitarianism and virtue ethics, this study develops a dual 
ethical lens to examine both the practical benefits of AI in banking and the mor-

2.1.2 Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT)  
The adoption of AI in banking is also a matter of technological acceptance and 
implementation at the organizational level. The Unified Theory of Acceptance 
and Use of Technology (UTAUT) (Venkatesh et al., 2003)  is a widely recognized 
framework for examining factors that influence technology acceptance  in the 
field of Information Systems . There are four major constructs , performance ex-
pectancy, effort expectancy, social influence and facilitating conditions in the  
model. This study applies UTAUT as a superficial  lens to analyse how AI adop-
tion in banking is shaped by organizational expectations, performance concerns, 
and ethical considerations.  
Specifically consider the  construct  performance expectancy  in above frame-
work  for this study  which refers to the perceived benefits and efficiency gains 
of adopting AI in banking. According to Lazo & Ebardo, (2023) , banks imple-
ment AI mostly to improve credit risk assessment, fraud detection, and auto-
mated customer service, with expectations that these technologies will enhance 
financial decision -making  in efficient and reliable manner . However, perfor-
mance -driven AI adoption must be balanced with ethical considerations such as 
bias mitigation, transparency, and fairness.  As an instance , while AI -driven 
credit scoring models may improve efficiency in loan approvals, they risk per-
petuating discrimination if trained on biased historical data. UTAUT helps ex-
plain why banks prioritize AI adoption for operational improvements while 
potentially overlooking ethical concerns, highlighting a critical tension that this 
study seeks to explore . 
Considering the construct effort efficiency , it refers the level of ease or per-
ceived usefulness related with the using of a particular system or technology . In 
this thesis, effort expectancy from UTAUT is employed to analyse how the per-
ceived ease of use of AI technologies in banking influences user acceptance 
across different stakeholders. It helps explain how ethical challenges  such as 
lack of transparency or complexity of AI systems  may increase perceived effort, 
thereby negatively affecting adoption. This construct links ethical concerns with 
user trust, accessibility, and willingness to engage with AI -driven banking solu-
tions.   
Another core construct of above framework is social influence  which refers 
to the degree to which individuals perceive that important other  (e.g., peers, 
supervisors, institutions) believe they should use a particular technology.  Ethi-
cal AI adoption in banking is highly dependent on public trust and regulatory 
compliance. If customers view AI  based decision  making as unfair or biased, 
banks face reputational risks and resistance to AI -driven financial services

2.1.3 Actor -Network Theory (ANT)  
The ANT  theory  which has been originated around 1970 and  has been widely 
expanding in many disciplines  is employed in the study  with relevance to both 
human and non -human actors (Bencherki, 2017; Callon, 1986; Callon & Law, 
1997)  in organizational context  to examine AI in banking as a socio -technical 
system. ANT conceptualizes both human and non -human entities : people, 
technologies, regulations, even data  as “actors” whose interactions form and 
transform dynamic networks.  
Latour (2005)  argues that to grasp how networks form and evolve, one 
must “follow the actors” through their negotiations, since the translation pro-
cess or how messages are interpreted, reshaped, and re ‑articulated ; fundamen-
tally determines what travels across a network.  It is vital to differentiate be-
tween interme diaries and mediators which have been manipulated in this study 
later. An intermediary  carries meaning or force from one point to another with-
out alteration, acting as a passive conduit  whereas a  mediator , by contrast, trans-
forms the message it carries  introducing unpredictability as actors reinterpret, 
reframe, or repurpose information within the network.  This concept will be uti-
lized in depth to discuss my framework  later in this study.  
Callon’s (1986)  four moments of translation ; problematization, inter-
essement, enrolment, and mobilization, are also been used to reveal how these 
networks co -construct what counts as knowledge, authority, or ethical practice.  
The four moments can be defined according to Callon as : Problematization : The 
phase in which a focal actor defines a problem and identifies other actors as in-
dispensable allies in addressing it.  Interessement : The process of deploying de-
vices or strategies to lock other actors into the roles outlined during problemati-
zation.  Enrolment : The negotiation and agreement among actors to accept, modi-
fy, or reject the roles assigned to them.  Mobilization : The stage where spokesper-
sons or representatives act on behalf of a broader network, solidifying collective 
engagement and action.  This concept in ANT developed by Callon , is signifi-
cant in depicting the process of translation of ethical use of AI in the banking 
ecosystem which is also discussed in -depth later in the study.  
AI can be considered as a socio -technical construct in the perspective of 
ANT which merely seemed to be a computational tool . Unlike frameworks that 
treat AI as an isolated technology, ANT considers AI as part of a network of 
human and non -human actors, including financial institutes, AI developers and 
regulatory institutions, customers or end users, and data and algorithmic mod-
els. This framework allows in -depth exploration of dynamic interactions and 
behaviours while adopting AI in banking context.  Due to the widespread use of

AI in current landscape, it is embedded within social, economic  and legal struc-
tures  inevitably.  ANT’s  theoretical lens  allows to study and analyse  how AI dy-
namically  interacts with other actors not only humans but also regulatory 
frameworks like GDPR and customer trust dynamics , how the translation of 
ethical ity among the varied networks would impact on holistic comprehension

ANT can be used to highlight the distributed nature of responsibility in AI 
adoption.  The ethical issues such as bias in credit approvals, privacy risks in 
data disclosure are not solely the responsibility of AI developers but also banks, 
regulators and policy developers are also included parties in it (Rahman et al.,

from a moral perspective but also considering the organizational and social im-
pacts on it. Utilization of multiple theoretical lenses also avoids author’s bias on 
analysis and provides a holistic framework  for comprehensive study.  
2.2 Research ques tions  
According to the existing knowledge in the domain, AI has been widely adopt-
ed while transforming the core banking operations to  digitalization along with 
enhanced efficiency and customer satisfaction.  But the ethical implications of 
the potential AI utilization are often underexplored and vaguely discussed in 
the existing literature.  
The problematization of this research was challenging, as the concept of 
“ethicality” is inherently context -dependent and lacks a universal definition. 
Furthermore, the tensions between the opportunities presented by AI and the 
potential ethical concerns highlight the need for a deeper examination of this 
issue.  
If legal frameworks were established to define ethical and non -ethical be-
haviour, AI developers could leverage these regulations to train models in a 
universally accepted manner.  Yet, such  universal framework could not be ob-
tained since the concept of ethicality varies depending on many contexts related 
to human behaviour  and even cultural and geographical foundations also. 
However, ethical principles such as fairness, equality, autonomy, veracity, con-
fidentiality, and fidelity ( Melé, 2024 ) are inherently complex and often lack 
clear -cut guidelines for implementation and evaluation. The absence of stand-
ardized measures to manage or quantify these ethical dimensions presents a 
significant challenge, making it difficult to navigate and enforce ethical AI de-

3.2 The concept ualization  of “ethical use” of AI  
“Ethics” is concerned when  discussing moral standards o f a social context or a 
community.  Ethics in economical ra tionality as ; “Ethics combined with logic, epis-
temology and metaphysics is a traditional cognitive sphere that originated in Ancient 
Greece ” (Baumane -Vitolina et al., 2016) . Accordingly , it may be regulatory or 
descriptive. A particular social group's existing norms and behavioural  stand-
ards are studied by descriptive ethics, which also aims to comprehend the pro-
cesses involved in ethical reasoning and decision -making. Regulatory ethics, on 
the other hand, uses logic and basic moral principles to theoretically examine 
moral standards.  Considering these theories in a technological context of adopt-
ing AI in a financial institute, once human intervention has been augmented  or 
automated  by a machine or an agent  (French & Shim, 2024) , the behavioural  
context covered by the human intervention must  be also considered in order to 
fill the gap properly.  In this adoption, AI can be used to automate  or assist the 
banking operations according to the requirement and nature of the tasks.  
Existing research acknowledges the ethical issues and challenges of utiliz-
ing AI in different contexts including banking and finance. But often demon-
strates a lack of systematic analysis of the scope , in relation to the ethical di-
mensions in depth . The major concern is that the norm of “ethical use” does not 
inherit a clear boundary hence it is massively dependent on the contextual ap-
plication  (Mudrack & Mason, 2013) . The rationale of “ethicality” is self -evident 
in the context it is embedded in  (Tenbrunsel et al., 2010) .  
Ethical principles vary significantly across individual beliefs, cultural con-
texts, and geographical boundaries. For instance, in many Asian societies, ethi-
cal considerations are deeply intertwined with religious and philosophical tra-
ditions, influencing daily life and decision -making. In contrast, Western cul-
tures often emphasize pragmatic benefits and individual autonomy over collec-
tive ethical concerns  (Peters & Carman, 2024) . This divergence highlights the 
complexity of defining "ethical behaviour " in a universally accepted manner. In 
the context of AI and machine learning, this complexity poses a significant chal-
lenge in developing algorithms that can accurately and robustly interpret ethi-

19 
cal principles. Since ethical behaviour  is inherently humanistic and context -
dependent, encoding it into AI systems requires nuanced approaches that ac-
count for cultural and societal differences  (Chernysh et al., 2024) . The absence of 
a universally accepted ethical framework further complicates AI training, mak-
ing it difficult to establish clear guidelines for what is considered ethical or un-
ethical across different communities worldwide  (Kurshan et al., 2022) .  
Throughout history, human behaviour  has evolved alongside technologi-
cal advancements. The discovery of fire led to transformative changes in early 
human lifestyles, while the invention of the wheel marked a significant mile-
stone in human progress. Similarly, disruptive technologies have consistently 
reshaped human civilization. Today, as we navigate an era dominated by artifi-
cial intelligence (AI), questions arise regarding the potential for AI to surpass 
human evolution. With the development of self -learning algorithms and com-
plex networking systems, AI has the capacity to influence and redefine human 
evolution in unprecedented ways  (Ridzuan et al., 2024) .  
The widespread adoption of AI raises critical ethical concerns, particularly 
in relation to human perceptions of what is considered right or acceptable ver-
sus wrong or unacceptable. Ethical theories, which have evolved over centuries, 
continue to play a fundamental role in societal decision -making, as humans re-
main central to ethical discourse. Theories such as Utilitarianism and Virtue 
Ethics provide valuable frameworks for assessing the ethical implications of AI 
adoption, ensuring alignment with established humanistic principles.  Accord-
ing to Baumane -Vitolina , Cals and Sumilo  (2016)  ethical theories are traditional-
ly categorized into three major groups: teleological, deontological, and virtue 
ethics. These frameworks provide insight into the potential positive and nega-
tive consequences of actions. Utilitarianism, a widely recognized teleological 
theory, has been extensively applied in economics to explain rational decision -
making. It assumes that efficiency and the pursuit of greater satisfaction under-
pin human morality. As articulated by Bentham, “The greatest happiness of the 
greatest number is the foundation of morality and legislation”  (Bentham, 1994) . This 
perspective emphasizes that ethical actions should consider the well -being of all 
affected parties.  Hence, utilization of particular technology should not negative-
ly impact upon the well -being of affected parties.  
In contrast  to Bentham , Baumane -Vitolina, Cals  and Sumilo (2016) argue 
that deontological theories advocate for the adherence to universal ethical prin-
ciples, regardless of specific outcomes. These theories highlight the significance 
of moral duty and the underlying intentions behind actions, rather than merely 
focusing on consequences. Deontological ethics assert that ethical behaviour  is a 
fundamental obligation rather than means to an end, reinforcing the necessity 
of ethical principles as guiding standards in decision -making . Virtue ethics o n 
top of the above  two types emphasizes that taking ethically correct decisions 
and presence of traits such as honesty, sense of responsibility and fairness, that 
impact  on goal achievement of certain social group or organization in a broader 
context.  The significance of adherence to ethical principles and regulations 
while adopting technology is timely important in the humanitarian perspective.

3.2.1 Privacy challenges  
Data privacy has become a significant concern in the current technological 
landscape due to the complexities and increasingly sophisticated means of ac-
cessing personal information, which is no longer solely individual but often 
indirectly shared (Kamleitner & Mitchell, 2019) . As individuals are intercon-
nected through various networks, their personal information becomes inter-
twined in a broader social context. Consequently, privacy has evolved into a 
multi -actor phenomenon, where individuals hold and passively share each oth-
er’s information , sometimes unintentionally . Hence, it has become an issue 
when the sensitive data has been shared and used without consent.  
In the context of AI adoption, data has emerged as a fundamental tech-
nical element. AI operates through three essential components: data gathering, 
data processing, and data output  (Martin & Zimmermann, 2024) . The most con-
troversial aspect of AI -driven data handling is that it processes vast amounts of 
information, including both intentionally provided data and non -intentionally 
provided data , such as digital footprints left behind through routine activities, 
including digital recognition systems and online tracking mechanisms. Re-
search has shown that certain uncontrollable factors undermine an individual's 
ability to rationally assess the decision to disclose personal data (Acquisti et al., 
2020; Kamleitner & Mitchell, 2019; Martin & Zimmermann, 2024) . From a 
broader perspective, it is sometimes inevitable that personal information is uti-
lized by third parties without explicit consent. AI’s capability to extract data 
from multiple sources further complicates this issue, raising concerns that the 
benefits of AI adoption may come at the expense of data privacy risks .  
The banks and financial institutions, which handle vast amounts of highly 
sensitive customer data, are particularly vulnerable to privacy breaches. For 
instance, Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly used in chatbots and 
virtual assistants for customer interactions.  As outlined in privacy policies, data 
is gathered through various channels, including direct user inputs, device and 
browser data such as IP addresses and locations, and even external sources 
such as social media and internet -synchronized databases (Mogaji & Nguyen, 
2022) . Given this extensive data collection, privacy risks in AI -powered banking 
operations are significantly higher compared to traditional banking systems.  
To address these concerns, the European Union’s General Data Protection 
Regulation (GDPR), which came into effect in 2018, has established mandatory 
requirements for businesses to ensure data protection and strengthen privacy 
safeguards for individuals (Kamaruddin et al., 2023) . GDPR enforces stringent 
guidelines on personal data processing, thereby providing a regulatory frame-
work that enhances data privacy protections .  
Financial institutions that have hesitated to integrate AI technologies into 
their operations , often cite data privacy risks and the potential for discriminato-
ry behaviour as primary barriers (Lazo & Ebardo, 2023) . However, privacy con-
cerns can be mitigated to some extent through effective regulatory frameworks, 
proper data processing protocols, and increased user awareness regarding per-

22 
sonal data management. The principles of privacy by design and privacy by 
default play a crucial role in ensuring data confidentiality and minimizing po-
tential harm  (Milossi et al., 2021) . Given the complexity of AI algorithms and 
logical frameworks, careful and strategic navigation is essential in designing 
AI-driven financial products that uphold ethical privacy standards . 
3.2.2 Transparency chall enges  
Transparency is widely recognized as a fundamental factor in establishing trust 
within society.  However, AI -driven decision -making is often characterized as a 
"black box" due to its inherent lack of transparency  (Gaurav & Tiwari, 2023) . 
Hanif et al., (2021) explain t his to be  an inherited characteristic of ML models 
which are highly beneficial in analysing  and processing large amount of data 
within least amount of time. In the context of AI, transparency  refers to the abil-
ity to understand how decisions are made, the rationale behind specific out-
comes, and the nature of the data utilized in the decision -making process. Hu-
mans inherently possess curiosity and a desire to comprehend their surround-
ings. When transparency is lacking, this fundamental need for understanding is 
left unfulfilled, ultimately leading to a decline in trust.  
Given the complexity and technical nature of AI, particularly for non -
technical individuals, there is an increasing demand for explanations regarding 
the solutions and decisions generated by AI systems  (Kurshan et al., 2022) . In 
the banking sector, transparency is particularly significant, as customers seek to 
understand the reasons behind critical financial decisions, such as loan rejec-
tions or declined transactions. Customers perceive access to such information as 
a justified right, expecting banks to provide clear explanations for AI -driven 
outcomes.  According to  the study by Kumar, Sharma and Mahdavi (2021) , since 
AI is extensively used for credit scoring, fraud detection, and risk management, 
the degree of transparency in its decision -making processes is a key concern for 
both customers and financial institutions.  The ethical challenge arises when AI 
is unable to offer a specific and comprehensible justification for a decision be-
yond stating that it is based on patterns identified in training data.   
AI models are increasingly utilized in credit scoring as part of the loan ap-
proval process  (Botunac et al., 2024) . However, even the developers of these 
models often struggle to fully explain how specific outcomes are generated, 
which factors exert the greatest influence on decisions, or whether these deci-
sions are made in a fair and justifiable manner. While Explainable AI (XAI) and 
Interpretable Machine Learning (IML) aim to enhance the transparency of AI -
driven decision -making, their practical implementation in the banking sector 
remains challenging due to technical constraints  (Deloitte Insights, 2022) . Con-
sequently, the ethical concern surrounding the lack of sufficient transparency in 
AI-based decision -making is a n overarching  critical issue in the adoption of AI 
within the banking industry.

3.2.3 Algorithmic bias related  challenges  
Bias in AI, often referred to as algorithmic bias, is a widely recognized issue in 
AI models. Since these models rely on the data used for training, human biases 
or inaccuracies in the data can perpetuate and even amplify discrimination, ste-
reotyping, and false -positive decision -making  (Waller et al., 2025) . Cheng, 
Varshney  and Liu in 2021  describe that b iases in training data may originate 
from existing social structures, such as racism, prejudice, and classism. Addi-
tionally, factors such as gender bias, socioeconomic bias, and unintended pref-
erences in input data can contribute to ethical dilemmas and discriminatory 
outcomes. Bias in AI development can also persist through a continuous cycle, 
where a lack of diversity among development teams influences how models are 
trained, further embedding systemic discrimination throughout data processing 
and output phases. In post -training stages, amplified biases may evolve into 
new, even more discriminatory behaviours  (Waller et al., 2025) .  
In the context of banking operations, Ridzuan et al. (2024) argue that bias 
must be eliminated in evaluating creditworthiness and determining access to 
financial services. Bias in AI -driven banking systems undermines fairness, po-
tentially marginalizing certain groups and eroding trust in financial institutions. 
If bias is not addressed, it may jeopardize an institution’s reputation and client 
confidence. Ideally, AI systems should surpass human decision -making in 
terms of fairness and impartiality. To achieve this, bias mitigation strategies 
must be implemented at both the training data and model development stages, 
ensuring that AI operates on accurate and unbiased data throughout the deci-
sion-making process.  
Bias in AI adoption within the banking sector presents a significant ethical 
challenge, as the primary objective of integrating AI is to minimize human er-
rors and enhance fairness in financial crediting and lending processes. However, 
research has demonstrated that AI -driven financial systems can inadvertently 
reinforce existing biases. For instance,  a study by  the National Bureau of Eco-
nomic Research  in 2019  found that AI -powered mortgage approval systems 
were more likely to deny  minority applicants co ntrasted  to equally qualified 
white applicants. The study also bared that Latinx and African American bor-
rowers were subjected to higher interest rates , 5.3 basis points higher for pur-
chase mortgages and 3.1 basis points higher for refinance mortgages  (Chopra, 
2024) . Similarly, instances of unfair insurance pricing and biased investment 
recommendations from AI powered robo -advisors have emerged as ethical con-
cerns in AI -driven banking operations  (Barile et al., 2024) . Addressing these 
challenges is crucial to ensuring that AI adoption in the financial sector aligns 
with principles of fairness and non -discrimination.

Accountability in AI adoption encompasses a broad definition of the obligation 
to inform, justify, and take responsibility for one’s conduct to relevant authori-

4 METHODOLOGY  
The research methodology employed in this study is the Systematic Literature 
Review (SLR), a scientific and structured approach to reviewing existing litera-
ture. SLR is a rigorous and comprehensive method designed to identify, evalu-
ate, and synthesize all relevant research on a specific topic or research question  
(Boell & Cecez -Kecmanovic, 2014, 2015) . Unlike a single case  or comparative 
case study  which provides deep but context -bound insights , an SLR synthesizes 
findings across multiple contexts ( such as  different banks, geographic regions 
and technical approaches) to reveal broader patterns in how ethical challenges 
of AI have been conceptualized and addressed.  Surveys or questionnaire ‐
based studies, by contrast, can capture perceptions at a single point in time but 
cannot systematically trace the evolution of academic discourse, nor can they 
correct for publication bias or methodological heterogeneity; the SLR ’s explicit 
inclusion/exclusion criteria, multi -database search strategy, and screening steps 
ensure that the literature sampling is both transparent and unbiased.  Experi-
mental or design -science approaches may demonstrate how a particular mitiga-
tion tool or ethical framework performs in practice, but they lack the capability 
to map the full landscape of existing solutions, identify latent theoretical gaps, 
or benchmark emerging practices against one another.  By aggregating 72 peer -
reviewed studies spanning 2018 -2025, this SLR not only provides a holistic and 
theory -driven categorization of ethical challenges but  also establishes a repro-
ducible roadmap for future researchers to update or e xtend this work as AI eth-
ics continues to evolve.  
To ensure rigor and transparency, a comprehensive search strategy was 
implemented, identifying peer -reviewed journal articles and conference papers 
from established academic databases. A clear search criterion, detailed later in 
the text , guided the selection process. The review adheres to strict inclusion and 
exclusion criteria, ensuring a focused and relevant study. Furthermore, as sys-
tematic literature reviews must be reproducible, the entire process has been 
carefully documented for future research and validation  using PRISMA dia-
gram (PRISMA Statement , 2020) . The temporal scope of this study spans 2018 –
2025, reflecting the significant increase in academic literature on AI ethics in

Considering the theoretical influence on the analysis, f rom the perspective

critically evaluates the extent to which AI adoption aligns with broader societal 
welfare and organizational ethics.

financial institutions uphold ethical responsibilities in AI -driven decision -
making. This theoretical perspective facilitated an examination of how AI mod-

When viewed in conjunction with ANT, each actor, be it customer, finan-

ing and being shaped by others. These interdependencies lead to varying inter-
pretations and ethical evaluations, particularly concerning data privacy, bias, 
transparency, and accountability.

rapid evolution of AI technologies, which complicates ethical evaluations and 
regulatory responses. As a result, this study establishes a distinct category for 
literature that acknowledges ethical issues at a general level, highlighting the 
need for more critical and detailed future research into ethical AI adoption in

6 DISCUSSION  
As the SLR findings demonstrate, the most salient ethical challenge in AI -
enabled banking is data privacy and security, an issue that cascades through, 
and indeed helps to instantiate, other core ethical concerns. From a utilitarian 
standpoint, violations of privacy generate substantial harm that may outweigh 
efficiency gains, while a virtue ethics lens highlights the erosion of trust and 
integrity when customer data is mishandled. These privacy breaches are not 
simply isolated failures but rather the products of translation processes: data 
flows (non -human actors) carry embedded interests and assumptions into algo-
rithms, where human actors (developers, bankers, regulators) may inadvertent-
ly reinforce biases or weaken accountability. Consequently, bias and discrimi-
nation become second -order ethical effects , symptoms of skewed data inscrip-
tions  while fairness, transparency, and explainability emerge as necessary me-
diators to realign network interests around shared ethical norms.  
These interlocking challenges directly undermine key adoption constructs  
from the viewpoint of UTAUT . Privacy and security concerns reduce perfor-
mance expectancy by casting doubt on AI’s reliability and benefit , they increase 
effort expectancy when users and staff must navigate complex consent or re-
mediation procedures; and they weaken social influence by eroding stakeholder 
trust in the institution’s commitment to responsible innovation. Each of these 
constructs can be seen as embedded within the  socio -technical network where 
ethical “messages” are translated , sometimes attenuated  across actors. The syn-
thesis of findings by SLR had led me to invent a new framework  promoting 
ethical AI adoption in banks which has been detailed in next sub -sections.  Then , 
I will discuss  on how stronger interessement process is significant rather than 
the implicit  declaration  led to clearer enrolment strategie s in technical and ex-
plicit perspectives , and more robust mobilization efforts  in law and regulation 
perspective  might realign stakeholder incentives and translate ethical principles 
into sustainable banking practices.  At the end of the discussion , future research 
directions are depicted to continue the studies on this important domain.

6.2 Significance of “translation process” in different networks  
The study reveals that “ethical challenges of AI adoption” do not exist as isolat-
ed or self -evident phenomena. Rather, these challenges emerge through the dy-
namic interplay between human actors, technological systems, regulatory struc-
tures, and institutional values. The findings indicate that ethical concerns are 
made visible only through the relationships and interactions that form within 
socio -technical networks. Most significantly, the academic literature reviewed 
in this study conveys varying degrees of engagement with ethical issues . The 
conceptual framework developed above has been impacted by this varied na-
ture o f narration by the ethical perspectives. Some articles identify ethics as a 
critical area requiring further investigation, while many merely acknowledge 
the presence of ethical concerns without substantive analysis. These articles 
function as "mentioners" invoking the language of ethics without engaging 
deeply with its implications. This superficial engagement represents a form of 
weak translation, as conceptualized by Michel Callon  in 1986 .  
Callon’s four moments of translation : problematization, interessement, en-
rolment, and mobilization  underscore the uneven and contested ways in which 
ethical imperatives are communicated across banking’s heterogeneous net-
works. Regulatory bodies and policymakers, for example, may establish high -
level ethical standards, but those norms must be translated through layers of AI 
developers, software integrators, and hardware vendors before they yield ac-
tionable guidance. In my framework , each perspective has their own networks 
and manipulate the ethical AI adoption in varied ways.  Each technical constitu-
ency “speaks” its own specialized language , algorithms, code repositories, de-
sign specifications , yet all must converge on a shared understanding of respon-
sible AI.  The efficacy of this translation, therefore, becomes central to ethical AI 
adoption . When only every network node internalizes and conveys the same 
core principles , then the industry  can collectively uphold data privacy, fairness, 
transparency, and accountability.  
Considering the studied pool or research articles  in this ANT perspective , 
the problematization  is often shallow, with few articles articulating a coherent 
or detailed framing of the ethical challenges associated with AI in banking.  As 
an example of implicit  or general  narration ; “As a number of our participants in the

6.3 Future research directions  
In light of the identified research gaps, several future research directions can be 
proposed to advance the understanding of ethical challenges associated with AI 
adoption in banking. Firstly, the lack of in -depth analysis of ethical challenges 
in existing literature highlights the need for longitudinal case studies of AI im-
plementation in banks. Such studies would offer empirical insights into how 
ethical guidelines are applied in real -world contexts and how organizations 
navigate associated challenges over time. Secondly, this study did not adopt a 
region -specific analytical approach. Therefore, comparative cross -regional stud-
ies, for instance, examining AI ethics in banking across the European Union and 
Asian countries  would be valuable in exploring how cultural, legal, and institu-
tional differences influence ethical challenges and governance frameworks.  
Moreover, although this study draws upon ethical theories such as utili-
tarianism and virtue ethics, there remains a clear gap in operationalizing these 
theoretical principles within the AI system development lifecycle. Future re-
search could explore how ethical theories can inform practical mechanisms such 
as bias audits, stakeholder engagement, and transparency protocols throughout 
system design and deployment. Additionally, with the increasing integration of 
large language models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT, i n financial services, further 
research is required to examine the ethical implications of generative AI, partic-

62 
ularly in relation to misinformation, informed consent, and transparency in AI -
human interactions.  
Finally, both qualitative and quantitative studies reviewed in this thesis 
often lacked depth in capturing the nuanced dimensions of ethical AI adoption. 
Future research should consider methodological innovations, including mixed -
methods approaches, participatory research, and action research frameworks, 
to better address the complex socio -technical nature of AI ethics in banking. 
These directions would not only fill existing academic gaps but also contribute 
to the development of more responsible and context -sensitive AI systems in the 
financial sector.  
 
Overall, the synthesis of existing literature clearly indicates that ensuring 
ethical AI adoption in banking requires an integrated approach that addresses 
data privacy and security as a foundation, while concurrently mitigating related 
issues of bias, fairness, accountability, transparency, and the preservation of 
essential human interactions.  The new  conceptual framework in promoting eth-
ical AI adoption in banks  highlights the importance of translations of the mes-
sage of “ethical AI  adoption ” within the collaboration of multiple stakeholder 
actor networks in order to promote sound norms and guidelines for ethical AI 
development and adoption in banks.

7 CONCLUSION  
In conclusion, this study systematically reviewed the ethical challenges associ-
ated with AI adoption in the banking sector, synthesizing insights from a di-
verse range of academic publications. The findings reveal that key ethical con-
cerns, particularly data privacy and security, algorithmic bias, transparency in 
AI decision -making, and accountability are frequently discussed across the lit-
erature. However, these discussions are often superficial, lacking in -depth anal-
ysis and practical guidance for mitigating risks.  Based on the findings, I have 
extrapolated four interrelated dimensions which then developed as a new 
framework promoting a conceptual approach for ethical AI adoption . Theoreti-
cal analysis revealed the significance of translation of the  message,  necessitating  
of ethical AI adoption in the banking sector .  
A notable limitation of this review is the inconsistent depth of analysis in 
the existing studies, which impedes a fully comprehensive understanding of 
how these ethical challenges interplay within the banking ecosystem. This un-
derscores a significant gap in the research, particularly regarding the operation-
alization of ethical principles in practical AI deployments.   
Future research should seek to address these gaps through more empiri-
cally rigorous studies that provide detailed, context -specific analyses of ethical 
AI adoption in banking. The novel framework can be referenced to study in -
depth how these four dimensions can be manipulated for ethical AI adoption 
with extended research. In particular, there is a need for research that develops 
robust metrics for evaluating ethical outcomes and examines the effectiveness 
of legal and regulatory frameworks in guiding responsible AI practices. Ad-
vances in these areas will not only enrich academic discourse but also support 
the development of ethically sound and accountable AI systems within the fi-
nancial sector.
